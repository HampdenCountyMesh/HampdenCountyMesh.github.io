A Community-Built Radio Network

Building Better Mesh Coverage

Small, well-placed radio nodes can help connect neighborhoods, towns, and local users across the difficult terrain of Western Massachusetts.

Hampden County Mesh is a volunteer, not-for-profit community project working to build useful MeshCore radio paths through cooperation, field testing, and legally hosted fixed nodes.

What We Are Building

A practical local network carried by radio

MeshCore devices exchange short messages directly or through fixed repeaters. The radio network can operate locally without requiring cellular service or an internet connection for every message.

Community communication Local users can exchange short messages through compatible radios and shared regional infrastructure.
Additional resilience Radio provides another communication path when ordinary cellular or internet service is unavailable, congested, or unreliable.
Technical learning The project gives community members practical experience with radio, antennas, electronics, mapping, solar power, and local infrastructure.
Regional cooperation Useful paths can connect independently operated nodes and neighboring mesh communities across Western Massachusetts and southern New England.
Hampden County Mesh is not a commercial communications provider and does not promise continuous coverage. It is a community-built network that improves as useful sites and participants are added.

Why Fixed Nodes Matter

One useful location can help many nearby users

A personal companion radio is used for messaging, but ordinary MeshCore companions do not relay traffic for the rest of the network. Wider coverage depends on separately operated repeaters placed where they can provide useful radio paths.

In Western Massachusetts, hills, valleys, buildings, trees, and dense neighborhoods can cause coverage to change quickly over short distances. Height and a clear path often matter more than simply using more transmitter power.

Height
Getting above nearby roofs, trees, and terrain can greatly increase the area a node can hear.
Placement
A good location can connect areas that otherwise cannot communicate directly.
Reliability
A fixed node that remains powered and in place provides a more consistent path than a portable radio that moves around.
Coordination
A smaller number of carefully placed nodes is generally more useful than many poorly positioned or redundant installations.

Small Radio Infrastructure

A hosted node does not need to be a radio tower

A typical fixed MeshCore node can consist of a compact radio inside a weather-resistant enclosure, a small antenna, and a power source.

Depending on the site, it may use a small solar panel and battery or nearby electrical power. A repeater does not need a site internet connection to relay ordinary MeshCore radio traffic.

Useful locations are not limited to commercial communications towers. An approved rooftop edge, upper-floor window, exterior railing, outbuilding, existing mast, fence post, or other elevated position may be enough.

Radio
A compact, low-power LoRa device running MeshCore repeater firmware.
Antenna
A small antenna selected and tested for the regional 915 MHz band.
Power
Solar and battery power or an agreed nearby electrical source, depending on the location.
Internet
Not required for the node’s basic radio-relay function.
Mounting
A site-specific bracket, strap, clamp, mast, or other approved mounting method.
Size
Usually much smaller and less visually prominent than traditional radio or telecommunications equipment.

Hosting a Node

Every possible site is discussed individually

HCM is not asking every interested organization for tower space or a large permanent installation. We are looking for legal, approved locations where a small radio node may provide a useful path.

Before anything is installed, the location, mounting method, power, access, equipment responsibility, maintenance, and removal expectations should be clear to everyone involved.

  1. Initial conversation The property owner or organization describes the general location, available height, access limitations, and any concerns.
  2. Site evaluation HCM reviews terrain, nearby activity, possible radio paths, power, sun exposure, mounting options, and whether testing is worthwhile.
  3. Simple installation plan Any proposed equipment, mounting method, access arrangement, and responsibilities are described before approval.
  4. Testing and review A temporary or trial installation may be used to confirm that the site actually provides useful coverage before treating it as a longer-term location.
Hosting a node does not open a property to the public. Any installation or maintenance access is arranged with the site owner, and equipment can be removed when a host no longer wishes to participate.

Practical Expectations

What a host may—and may not—need to provide

A possible location

The most valuable contribution may simply be permission to evaluate an elevated or unobstructed part of the property.

Occasional arranged access

Maintenance or inspection may require scheduled access, depending on where the equipment is installed.

No daily operation

A host should not normally need to operate, monitor, or administer the radio as part of ordinary participation.

No site internet account

A normal fixed repeater can relay radio traffic without using the host’s internet service.

Power may be optional

Sites with adequate sun exposure may support solar operation. Building power is useful in some locations but is not always needed.

No commercial lease

HCM is a volunteer community project and does not operate a paid tower-leasing or site-rental program.

Possible Locations

Many different properties may have a useful site

A suitable location does not need to be the tallest structure in the region. A modest building or property in the right place may fill an important gap between existing radio paths.

Homes and apartment buildings Upper floors, balconies, roof edges, garages, sheds, and existing antenna mounts.
Businesses and office buildings Rooftops, mechanical areas, railings, exterior structures, or other approved elevated locations.
Nonprofits and community organizations Community centers, clubs, meeting spaces, houses of worship, and organizations interested in local communication projects.
Schools, libraries, and municipal properties Locations where the appropriate organization and property authority are interested and able to approve a small installation.
Farms and larger private properties Barns, outbuildings, ridgelines, clear fields, existing masts, and other sites with useful elevation or visibility.
Radio and technical sites Amateur-radio properties, communications facilities, towers, and existing equipment locations where a small community node is appropriate.

Local Testing

Maps identify possibilities; radio testing confirms them

Terrain maps, elevation data, and observed network activity can help identify promising areas. Actual testing is still needed because buildings, trees, antenna placement, interference, and local terrain can affect the result.

HCM uses portable radios, contributed observations, MeshMapper, and its activity tools to better understand which locations may connect existing nodes or fill meaningful gaps.

Common Questions

Before discussing a possible site

Does a node need internet service?

No. A normal MeshCore repeater relays radio packets without needing the host’s internet connection. Separate observer or mapping integrations may use internet service, but those are not required for a basic repeater.

Does it need building power?

Not always. Some locations can use a small solar panel and battery. Other sites may be better suited to an agreed low-power electrical connection.

Does the property need to have a tower?

No. A rooftop edge, railing, upper-floor window, outbuilding, existing mast, fence post, or other approved position may be useful.

Is an installation necessarily permanent?

No. A temporary or trial installation may be used first, and removable mounting is preferred where practical. Longer-term arrangements are discussed with the host before installation.

Who supplies and maintains the equipment?

That is decided for each site. HCM discusses equipment ownership, installation, maintenance, access, and removal responsibilities before anything is placed on the property.

Does hosting provide public access to the property?

No. Access remains under the control of the property owner or organization. Any maintenance visit is arranged in advance.

Does HCM pay rent for node locations?

No. Hampden County Mesh is a volunteer, not-for-profit community project rather than a commercial telecommunications provider or paid site-leasing program.

Does a node guarantee coverage?

No. Coverage depends on terrain, antenna placement, surrounding structures, other active nodes, and radio conditions. That is why HCM evaluates and tests locations before relying on them.
