Documenting a Node

A node is more useful when people understand what it is, what role it plays, how it is powered, where it generally operates, and what it can hear. Good notes turn one device into useful local knowledge.

Do not publish private addresses, exact private coordinates, keys, passwords, broker credentials, Wi-Fi details, private admin URLs, or sensitive locations. For public notes, a general area is usually enough.

Why documentation matters

Mesh radio is not just hardware. It is also notes, testing, maintenance, local knowledge, and people who understand how the pieces fit together.

If a node works well, documentation helps others learn from it. If a node fails, documentation helps people troubleshoot it. If a node moves, changes firmware, changes antenna, or goes offline, notes help explain what changed.

This is especially useful for fixed nodes, repeaters, observers, solar nodes, community-hosted equipment, and temporary setups used during signal checks.

What counts as a node?

For documentation, “node” can mean a few different things. The exact label matters less than clearly explaining the role.

Portable node

A handheld, pocket, vehicle, or temporary device used for field testing, messaging, or moving around the county.

Fixed node

A device that usually stays in one place, such as a window, workplace, club, library, garage, or other regular location.

Repeater

A device placed or configured to help traffic move farther over radio. Repeaters depend heavily on placement and reliability.

Observer

A device that listens for activity and helps document what can be heard from a location. An observer improves visibility, not necessarily radio coverage.

Room server

A MeshCore support role used for room-style messaging and, in some setups, more permanent local infrastructure.

Hub or support system

A computer, script, logger, broker, dashboard, or other backend system that supports local mesh work.

Minimum useful information

A basic node note does not need to be fancy. Start with the details someone else would need to understand what the device is doing.

  • Node name.
  • Device model.
  • System: MeshCore, Meshtastic, or other.
  • Role: portable node, fixed node, repeater, observer, room server, or hub.
  • General area or type of location.
  • Antenna type and placement.
  • Power source.
  • Firmware or software version, if known.
  • Whether it is indoor, outdoor, mobile, fixed, temporary, or experimental.
  • What it can hear or reach.
  • Known problems or maintenance needs.
  • Last time it was checked.

If you do not know every detail, write down what you do know. Partial notes are better than no notes.

MeshCore notes

For MeshCore devices, it is useful to record the role clearly. A companion device, repeater, observer, and room server do not all do the same thing.

  • Device name used on the mesh.
  • Device model, such as a Heltec, Wio Tracker, or SenseCAP device.
  • Role, such as companion, repeater, observer, or room server.
  • Firmware build or version, if known.
  • General radio settings or preset, if useful and safe to share.
  • General area where it is used or heard.
  • Observed activity, reachable nodes, or signal check notes.
  • Whether a result came from direct radio, an observer, MQTT, or a dashboard.

For public-facing notes, avoid going too deep into backend details. Most people need to know what the node does, where it generally helps, and whether it is active or being tested.

Meshtastic notes

For Meshtastic devices, useful notes may include the device model, firmware, region, channel context, antenna, placement, role, and whether it is portable or fixed.

  • Device model and firmware version.
  • Region and channel context, if appropriate to share.
  • Role or intended use.
  • Antenna and power setup.
  • General operating area.
  • Known nodes heard or reached.
  • Whether maps, MQTT, or internet-connected paths were involved.

Be thoughtful about privacy settings and public maps. Not every device needs to expose its exact location.

Location and privacy

Useful documentation does not require exposing private addresses. For most public notes, use a general location description.

Usually okay for public notes

  • Town or general area.
  • Public landmark area.
  • “Near Mount Tom,” “downtown Westfield,” or “Springfield riverfront.”
  • “Residential window,” “tree-mounted,” “vehicle roof,” or “library parking lot.”
  • General terrain notes, such as hill, valley, wooded area, or dense buildings.

Avoid posting publicly

  • Exact home addresses.
  • Exact private coordinates.
  • Someone else’s workplace or residence.
  • Private keys, passwords, or broker credentials.
  • Wi-Fi names, Wi-Fi passwords, private IP addresses, or admin URLs.
  • Photos that reveal private details unintentionally.
  • Access instructions for private property or equipment.

The goal is to understand local coverage and device roles without making people uncomfortable or exposing more than necessary.

Photos

Photos can help with antennas, mounting, field checks, and hardware setups. They also need a little care.

Useful photos

  • Device on a workbench.
  • Antenna setup.
  • Solar or battery setup.
  • General public field check location.
  • Public hilltop, park, or terrain view.
  • Safe example of a clean installation.

Before posting photos

  • Check that the photo does not reveal a private address.
  • Remove location metadata when needed.
  • Avoid showing keys, passwords, private screens, or personal details.
  • Check reflections, license plates, mail, signs, and screen contents.
  • Do not include people unless they agreed to be included.
  • Use a general caption that explains why the photo matters.

A good photo should help someone understand the setup, not expose more information than intended.

Maintenance notes

Fixed nodes, repeaters, observers, and support systems need occasional checking. A node that worked last month may not still be working now.

  • Last date checked.
  • Battery or power status.
  • Weather damage or mounting issues.
  • Firmware changes.
  • Antenna changes.
  • Observed traffic or lack of traffic.
  • Anything that needs repair, replacement, or retesting.

Sharing node notes

Node notes can be shared in Discord, kept in GitHub documentation, saved locally, or summarized on public coverage pages.

Keep public notes readable. Deep technical logs can be useful, but most visitors need a plain summary first: what the node is, what role it plays, and whether it helps local mesh use.

Good first goals

  • Write a short note for one device you already use.
  • Add the model, role, antenna, power, and general location.
  • Record the last time it was checked.
  • Share one useful signal result.
  • Take one safe, non-private photo of the setup.
  • Update the note when the device moves, changes antenna, or changes firmware.

Start with simple notes. Documentation can get more detailed as the local community learns what is useful.

Ask the community

If you are unsure what details are useful, ask in Discord. A rough note can be cleaned up later, and it is better to ask before posting private information.
