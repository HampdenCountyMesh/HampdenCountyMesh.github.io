Glossary

A plain-language reference for common radio, mesh, MeshCore, Meshtastic, LoRa, signal check, mapping, privacy, and local communications terms.

This glossary is meant for beginners. Some terms may be used differently depending on firmware, device settings, radio service, local habits, or official rules.

Basic radio terms

Frequency

The specific radio signal rate being used, usually measured in hertz. Radio users often talk in kilohertz, megahertz, or gigahertz.

Band

A range of frequencies grouped together. Examples include HF, VHF, UHF, CB, GMRS, amateur radio bands, Wi-Fi bands, and the 900 MHz area used by many LoRa devices in the United States.

Bandwidth

The amount of radio spectrum used by a signal. In LoRa systems, bandwidth affects speed, range, airtime, and how easily a signal can be received.

RX / receive

RX means receive. It refers to hearing, detecting, or receiving a signal, packet, message, or transmission.

TX / transmit

TX means transmit. It refers to sending a signal, packet, message, or transmission.

Channel

A channel may mean a frequency, a programmed radio memory, or a configured communication path. In Meshtastic, a channel can also mean a configured message group with a name, key, and settings.

Modulation

The way information is carried on a radio signal. AM, FM, SSB, digital voice, packet data, and LoRa all use different methods.

AM

AM means amplitude modulation. It carries information by changing the strength of the radio wave.

FM

FM means frequency modulation. It carries information by changing the frequency of the radio wave.

SSB

SSB means single sideband. It is a voice mode used on HF amateur radio and some CB radios. It is more efficient than regular AM, but both sides need to be tuned carefully.

Analog

A signal that varies continuously, such as traditional AM or FM voice.

Digital

A signal or mode that carries information as data. MeshCore, Meshtastic, DMR, packet radio, and FT8 are examples of digital radio uses.

Dead air

A period when nothing is being transmitted or heard on a channel.

Voice radio terms

PTT / push to talk

The button or control used to transmit on many voice radios.

Squelch

A setting that keeps a radio speaker quiet until a signal is strong enough or matches the expected tone or code.

CTCSS

CTCSS means Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System. It uses a low audio tone to open squelch on radios set to the same tone. These are sometimes marketed as privacy codes, but they do not make the channel private.

DCS

DCS means Digital-Coded Squelch. It works like a digital squelch code. Like CTCSS, it does not encrypt the channel.

Simplex

Direct radio-to-radio communication without a repeater.

Duplex

Communication that uses separate transmit and receive frequencies. Repeaters commonly use duplex operation.

Voice repeater

A radio system that receives a voice transmission and retransmits it, usually from a better location or higher site. This is different from a mesh repeater, even though both can help extend communication.

Repeater offset

The difference between the frequency a radio receives on and the frequency it transmits on when using a repeater.

DMR

DMR means Digital Mobile Radio. It is a digital voice radio standard used in commercial, public-service, and amateur radio contexts. In amateur radio, DMR often involves repeaters, talkgroups, time slots, and radio IDs.

CW / Morse code

CW means continuous wave. In amateur radio, CW usually refers to Morse code sent by turning a carrier signal on and off.

FT8

A weak-signal digital mode used by amateur radio operators, usually with a computer handling timing and decoding.

Net

A scheduled or organized radio gathering where stations check in and exchange information.

Net control

The person or station helping organize a radio net. Net control may call for check-ins, keep the net moving, and help traffic stay clear.

Call sign

An official or assigned station identifier. Amateur radio operators use FCC-issued call signs in the United States.

Mesh and packet terms

Mesh

A network where devices can communicate with each other and may pass messages through other devices instead of relying on one central tower, router, or service.

Node

A device that participates in a mesh. It may be handheld, portable, vehicle-mounted, fixed indoors, fixed outdoors, battery-powered, or solar-powered.

Node ID

A unique identifier used by a device or system to tell one node from another. A node ID may be different from the friendly name people see in an app or on a map.

Portable node

A node that moves around with a person, vehicle, bag, or temporary test setup.

Fixed node

A node that usually stays in one place, such as a window, shelf, rooftop, garage, club space, or other regular location.

Repeater

A node placed or configured to help messages travel farther. Repeaters usually benefit from height, good antenna placement, reliable power, and weather protection.

Store-and-forward

A method where a system receives a message, keeps it for some period of time, and forwards or delivers it later. The exact behavior depends on the software or network.

Observer

A device or system that listens for mesh activity and may feed logs, analyzers, dashboards, maps, or status tools. An observer improves visibility, but does not necessarily improve radio coverage by itself.

Room server

A MeshCore support role used for room-style messaging and, in some setups, more permanent local support.

Gateway or bridge

A system that connects one network or tool to another, such as a radio mesh to an internet dashboard, logger, broker, or chat tool.

Uplink

A path that sends local data up to another system, such as an analyzer, broker, dashboard, or other public feed. An uplink does not automatically mean every nearby device is operated by the same group.

Packet

A small unit of digital data sent across a network or radio link.

Hop

One forwarding step in a mesh network.

Hop limit

A setting that controls how many times a message may be forwarded. Higher hop limits may reach farther, but can also create more radio traffic.

TTL / time to live

A limit on how far or how long a message can travel before it is dropped.

ACK / acknowledgement

A confirmation that a message or packet was received.

Direct message

A message intended for a specific person or node instead of a shared public channel or group.

Telemetry

Status data reported by a device, such as battery level, temperature, uptime, location, signal information, or other device health details.

MQTT

A lightweight messaging protocol often used to move status, sensor, or mesh-related data between systems. MQTT can be useful for logs, dashboards, analyzers, and bridge tools.

Broker

A server that receives and distributes MQTT messages.

Region

A defined area or grouping used to organize network behavior, mapping, routing, or reporting.

Scope

A setting or concept that controls how far a message or operation is intended to reach.

LoRa, MeshCore, and Meshtastic terms

LoRa

LoRa means long range. It is a low-power radio technology used for sending small data packets over longer distances than many short-range wireless systems.

LoRaWAN

LoRaWAN is a network system built on LoRa radio technology. It is commonly used for sensor networks and internet-connected gateways. MeshCore and Meshtastic use LoRa radios, but they are not the same thing as LoRaWAN.

MeshCore

A LoRa-based mesh communication system used here for local experimentation, repeaters, observers, room servers, signal checks, and learning what works around Hampden County.

Meshtastic

A separate LoRa-based mesh communication project commonly used for off-grid text messaging, position sharing, telemetry, and hobby or community mesh networks.

LongFast

A common Meshtastic preset/channel name associated with longer-range, lower-speed communication. Local practice can vary.

ShortFast

A Meshtastic preset associated with shorter-range, faster communication compared with longer-range presets.

Spread factor / SF

A LoRa setting that affects range, speed, airtime, and how easily a signal can be decoded. Higher spread factors usually favor longer range but use more airtime.

915 MHz

A radio range commonly associated with LoRa and ISM-style operation in the United States and some other regions. Rules vary by country and use.

868 MHz

A radio range commonly associated with LoRa and ISM-style operation in parts of Europe and other regions. Rules vary by country and use.

Firmware

The software installed on a radio device or microcontroller.

Flashing

Installing or replacing firmware on a device.

OTA / over the air

Sending updates or configuration wirelessly instead of using a USB cable. The exact meaning depends on the device or system.

BLE / Bluetooth Low Energy

A short-range wireless technology often used for phone-to-device connections, configuration, sensors, and accessories.

API

API means application programming interface. It is a way for software tools to talk to each other.

Antennas and RF measurements

Antenna

The part of a radio system that sends or receives radio signals.

Rubber duck antenna

A short flexible antenna commonly used on handheld radios. It is convenient, but usually not the best-performing antenna.

Whip antenna

A straight or flexible antenna often used on handheld, mobile, or portable equipment.

Dipole antenna

A simple antenna made from two conductive elements. Dipoles are common because they are useful, simple, and easy to understand.

Omnidirectional antenna

An antenna designed to send or receive signal in all horizontal directions around the antenna.

Yagi antenna

A directional antenna that focuses signal more strongly in one direction.

Coax / feed line

The cable that carries radio-frequency energy between a radio and an antenna.

Connector

The physical plug or jack used to connect radios, antennas, adapters, and coax.

Ground plane

A conductive surface or set of radials that helps some antennas work properly.

Polarization

The orientation of a radio wave or antenna, commonly vertical or horizontal. Matching polarization can improve signal performance.

Gain

A measure of how an antenna focuses signal in some directions compared with others.

dBi

Decibels relative to an isotropic radiator. It describes antenna gain compared with a theoretical antenna that radiates equally in every direction.

dBd

Decibels relative to a dipole antenna. It describes antenna gain compared with a standard half-wave dipole.

dBm

Decibels relative to one milliwatt. It is often used to describe radio power levels and received signal strength.

SWR

Standing wave ratio. It describes how well an antenna system is matched to the radio and feed line. Very high SWR can reduce performance and may damage some transmitters.

Signal quality and range

RSSI

Received Signal Strength Indicator. It is a measurement of how strong a received signal appears to the radio.

SNR

Signal-to-noise ratio. It compares the desired signal against background noise. A better SNR usually means the signal is easier to decode.

Noise floor

The background radio noise level. A weak signal can be hard to use if it is too close to the noise floor.

Duty cycle

The percentage of time a radio transmits over a period of time. Limiting airtime is good practice on shared radio channels.

Airtime

The amount of time a transmission occupies the channel. Long airtime can reduce how much room is available for other traffic.

Line of sight

A mostly clear path between radio devices. Hills, buildings, trees, and terrain still matter even when devices are close together.

Terrain

The physical landscape around a radio path, including hills, valleys, buildings, trees, rivers, roads, and dense neighborhoods.

Coverage

The area where a device, repeater, observer, or system may be heard or used. Coverage is often incomplete and can change with placement, terrain, antenna, power, and time.

Dead spot

An area where a device does not hear or reach expected traffic.

Bands and services

HF

High frequency. HF covers 3 to 30 MHz and can support long-distance communication under some conditions.

VHF

Very high frequency. VHF covers 30 to 300 MHz and is commonly used for local and regional radio communication.

UHF

Ultra high frequency. UHF covers 300 MHz to 3 GHz and is used by many handheld radios, repeaters, data links, and local systems.

FRS

Family Radio Service. A short-range personal radio service commonly used by small handheld radios.

GMRS

General Mobile Radio Service. A personal radio service often used for handhelds, mobile radios, base stations, family communication, and repeaters.

MURS

Multi-Use Radio Service. A VHF personal radio service with a small channel set.

CB

Citizens Band. A personal radio service commonly associated with 27 MHz AM and SSB voice communication.

Amateur radio / ham radio

A licensed radio service for technical learning, experimentation, public service, and communication.

Scanner

A receiver used to listen to radio traffic. A scanner does not transmit.

ISM band

Industrial, scientific, and medical band. ISM bands are shared radio ranges used by many devices, including some LoRa systems.

Wi-Fi

A local wireless networking technology commonly used for internet access and local networks.

Satellite

A radio relay or payload in orbit. Some amateur radio satellites can repeat voice or digital signals between ground stations.

Security, keys, and privacy

End-to-end encryption

A way of protecting messages so only the intended endpoints should be able to read them.

AES-256

Advanced Encryption Standard using 256-bit keys. Encryption strength also depends on how keys are generated, shared, stored, and protected.

Public key

A cryptographic key that can be shared. Other devices or users may use it to identify a node or send encrypted information to it, depending on the system.

Private key

A cryptographic key that should be kept secret. Do not post private keys publicly.

Channel key

A key used by some systems to control who can read or participate in a channel. Treat channel keys as private unless they are meant to be public.

Broker credentials

Usernames, passwords, or tokens used to connect to an MQTT broker or other backend system. Do not post broker credentials publicly.

Admin URL

A private web address or local address used to manage a device, server, router, dashboard, or backend tool. Do not post admin URLs publicly unless they are intentionally public.

Metadata

Information about a message, device, file, or photo. Metadata may include timestamps, location, device names, or other details even when the main content looks harmless.

EXIF data

Metadata stored inside some photos. EXIF data can include camera details and sometimes location information. Remove it before sharing sensitive photos publicly.

Field testing and maps

Signal check

A practical check to see what a device can hear or reach from a specific general area.

Coverage test

A broader check of where a device, repeater, observer, or system may be heard or used. On this site, “signal check” is often the plainer term because most local tests are snapshots, not complete coverage maps.

Wardriving

Driving around with a device or logging setup to observe radio or mesh activity over an area. It should be done safely, without interacting with devices while driving.

Field note

A short observation from real use, such as what device was used, where it was tested in general terms, what was heard, what did not work, and what terrain or placement mattered.

Map

A visual tool for showing places, nodes, routes, observed activity, or signal check notes. A map point does not always prove direct radio reachability.

Dashboard

A web page or tool that summarizes status, activity, logs, nodes, or other data.

Live activity

Recently observed activity shown on a map, analyzer, dashboard, or status page. Live activity does not mean the map is complete.

Observed activity

Activity heard or reported by an observer, public feed, analyzer, log, or community report.

Site-maintained system

A device, server, page, tool, or service maintained directly by the people running this site. Not every node, packet, or marker seen in the area is site-maintained.

Public note

A note meant to be shared publicly. It should be useful while avoiding private addresses, exact private coordinates, keys, passwords, and other sensitive details.
