Hosting a Meetup

A useful mesh or radio meetup does not need to be large or formal. A few people, a table, a couple of devices, clear expectations, and time for questions can be enough.

Hampden County Mesh meetups should be realistic and beginner-friendly. Do not promise emergency coverage, guaranteed communications, or official public safety capability.
Meetup Types Before You Plan Location Basic Kit Simple Flow After

Who this is for

This guide is for someone who wants to host a small meetup at a library, club meeting room, makerspace, school, community center, ham radio club, public-friendly meeting space, or informal outdoor location.

The host does not need to be an expert. A useful meetup can be as simple as showing a node, explaining the basic idea, answering questions, and helping people understand what to try next.

It is also fine for a first meetup to be small. A small group with good questions can be more useful than a large event with too many topics.

Good meetup types

What is mesh?

A beginner-friendly explanation of MeshCore, Meshtastic, LoRa radios, local terrain, and how mesh networks work.

Radio basics

A broad introduction to FRS, GMRS, MURS, CB, amateur radio, scanners, MeshCore, and Meshtastic.

Get your hands on a node

A casual demo where people can see a device, ask questions, and understand portable nodes, repeaters, and observers.

Try a node

A short indoor, outdoor, or mixed meetup where people can see how placement, antennas, and local terrain affect small radios.

Build or setup help

A more technical meetup for people with devices who want help with firmware, apps, antennas, power, or basic setup.

Show-and-tell

A low-pressure meetup where people bring devices, antennas, batteries, cases, mounts, or ideas to discuss.

Before you plan

Start small. A first meetup does not need a full presentation, a large audience, or a perfect equipment list.

  • Pick one clear topic.
  • Choose a public-friendly location.
  • Decide whether the meetup is beginner, technical, or mixed.
  • Check whether power, internet, tables, parking, and outdoor space are available.
  • Bring only equipment you are comfortable explaining.
  • Know what is allowed before bringing antennas, tripods, soldering tools, batteries, or extension cords.
  • Avoid promising coverage, emergency capability, or guaranteed results.

A simple meetup with honest limits is better than an overplanned meetup that depends on everything working perfectly.

Choosing a location

Good locations are accessible, legal to use, comfortable for beginners, and appropriate for the kind of meetup being held.

Libraries, club rooms, makerspaces, schools, community centers, and ham radio club meetings can all work. Outdoor public places can be useful for trying devices, but they need more attention to weather, parking, safety, permission, and visibility.

Ask about the basics

  • Is the space open to the public or invite-only?
  • Are tables and chairs available?
  • Are power outlets available?
  • Is internet access available?
  • Are antennas, tripods, laptops, chargers, or extension cords allowed?
  • Is there a safe outdoor area nearby?
  • Are there rules about photos, recording, signs, or handouts?
  • Is the space accessible for people with mobility needs?
  • Is parking or public transportation reasonable for attendees?

Permissions and expectations

Before using a space, make sure the host or property owner understands what the meetup is. Explain that it is a learning meetup about mesh radio and local communications, not an emergency service or official public safety program.

  • Ask before putting up signs, antennas, tripods, or temporary outdoor equipment.
  • Ask before taking photos or recording.
  • Follow the location’s rules for power strips, batteries, tools, and cables.
  • Keep walkways clear and avoid trip hazards.
  • Do not leave equipment unattended unless the host has agreed.
  • Do not install anything permanently without clear permission.

Basic host kit

A meetup can be run with very little equipment. Bring what you have and be honest about what is experimental.

  • One or two mesh devices for demonstration.
  • A phone or laptop used to show basic apps or messages.
  • Chargers, USB cables, and a small power strip if allowed.
  • A printed or written link to the website and Discord join route.
  • Optional small antennas, cases, mounts, batteries, or example parts.
  • Optional notebook for common questions.
  • Optional one-page handout for beginners.
Avoid making the meetup depend on one fragile live demo. If a demo fails, the meetup can still be useful as a discussion and learning event.

Simple meetup flow

This structure works well for a first meetup.

  1. Welcome people and explain the purpose of the meetup.
  2. Say clearly that mesh radio is not a replacement for 911.
  3. Give a plain-language overview of MeshCore, Meshtastic, and local radio.
  4. Show one or two real devices.
  5. Explain the difference between a portable node, repeater, and observer.
  6. Talk about local terrain, antennas, and placement.
  7. Answer questions.
  8. Share the website and Discord join route.
  9. Write down common questions or useful follow-up ideas.

Keeping it beginner-friendly

People may show up with very different backgrounds: radio hobbyists, IT people, makers, students, hikers, preparedness-minded neighbors, scanner listeners, or people who are simply curious.

  • Explain terms plainly.
  • Let people ask basic questions.
  • Be clear when something is experimental.
  • Do not make people feel behind because they do not know the jargon.
  • Do not turn every beginner question into a deep technical lecture.
  • Point people to guides they can read later.

A good first meetup should make people more comfortable asking the next question.

Safety, privacy, and expectations

Keep expectations realistic. Mesh radio is useful for learning, local communication practice, and off-grid messaging experiments. It is not an official emergency service.

  • Do not publish private addresses or sensitive locations without permission.
  • Do not pressure attendees to share exact locations, phone numbers, call signs, or personal details.
  • Use general location descriptions in public discussion when possible.
  • Follow the rules of the space hosting the meetup.
  • Follow applicable radio rules for the service or device being used.
  • Do not represent experimental results as guaranteed coverage.
  • Do not take or post photos of people without permission.
  • Do not handle devices while driving during any mobile use.

Photos and notes

Photos and notes can help preserve what happened at a meetup, but they should be handled carefully.

Useful notes

  • What topic was covered.
  • What questions people asked.
  • What devices or antennas were shown.
  • What guide or handout would help next time.
  • What setup questions came up repeatedly.

Before sharing photos

  • Did people in the photo agree to be included?
  • Does the photo show a private address, plate, badge, screen, or reflection?
  • Does the photo contain location metadata?
  • Does the photo reveal a private node location?
  • Would a cropped or more general photo be better?

Promoting a meetup

Keep public descriptions simple and accurate. Say what the meetup is, who it is for, whether beginners are welcome, and whether people need to bring hardware.

  • Use plain language.
  • Say whether people need a device. Usually, they should not need one.
  • Say whether the meetup is beginner-friendly.
  • Do not advertise it as emergency communications training unless that is truly what is being hosted.
  • Do not imply guaranteed coverage or official public safety capability.

After the meetup

A short follow-up makes the meetup more useful.

  • Write down what was covered.
  • Note common questions people asked.
  • Share useful links with attendees.
  • Identify what guide, handout, or demo would help next time.
  • Invite interested people to continue in Discord.
  • Update the website or guides if the meetup exposed a confusing gap.

Sample meetup description

This can be adapted for a library, club, community room, or informal meetup listing. 

Join Hampden County Mesh for an informal introduction to mesh radio and local off-grid messaging.

We will talk about MeshCore, Meshtastic, basic radio ideas, small LoRa devices, antennas, local terrain, and how people nearby can get involved. Beginners are welcome. You do not need to own a device to attend.

This is a learning and discussion meetup, not an emergency service or public safety program.

Short invite text

Use this when you need a shorter version for a post, flyer, or event blurb. 

Curious about mesh radio, MeshCore, Meshtastic, or local off-grid messaging?

This informal beginner-friendly meetup will explain the basic idea, show a few devices, and talk about antennas, terrain, and how to get started. No device required.
