Coverage Testing

Coverage testing means checking what can be heard from real places. A useful test does not need to be complicated. The goal is to turn one person’s trip, walk, drive, or field session into notes that help the whole community understand the local network.

Coverage reports do not need to expose exact home addresses or private locations. A general area, nearby landmark, road, park, neighborhood, or public place is usually enough for community notes.

Why Coverage Testing Matters

Radio coverage is shaped by the real world. Hills, valleys, trees, buildings, antenna height, device placement, and weather can all change what works.

Hampden County has a mix of wooded hills, dense city streets, river corridors, old industrial buildings, campuses, hospitals, highways, parks, and rural edges. A device that works well in one place may behave very differently a few miles away.

Field testing helps turn guesses into shared local knowledge. When people write down what they tested and what happened, others can make better decisions about nodes, repeaters, antennas, and future test locations.

A Simple Test

A basic test can be as simple as taking a node or radio to a public location and writing down what it can hear.

  • Pick a safe place to test from.
  • Note the general location.
  • Turn on the device and let it run long enough to hear activity.
  • Record what nodes, repeaters, stations, or signals were heard.
  • Write down the device, antenna, power, and any important settings.
  • Share the result with the community.

The test does not have to be perfect. A short, honest note is more useful than waiting forever for a perfect report.

What to Record

The most useful reports include enough detail that someone else can understand the result later.

Location

Use a general location unless there is a good reason to be more specific. Examples: “Stanley Park parking area,” “downtown Westfield,” “near Mt Tom,” “Springfield riverfront,” or “public library parking lot.”

Date and time

Include the date and approximate time. If you know UTC, include it. If not, local time is fine.

Device and role

Note what you used: handheld radio, MeshCore node, Meshtastic node, observer, repeater, mobile setup, or another device.

Antenna and placement

Antenna details matter. A small antenna inside a car, a mag-mount on a roof, a handheld at chest height, and a fixed antenna up high can all perform very differently.

What was heard

Record node names, station calls, signal reports, message activity, logs, or anything else that helps describe what was reachable.

What did not work

Failed tests are useful. If nothing was heard, say that. If one area was dead but another nearby spot worked, that is worth documenting.

Good Places to Test

Good test locations are places that teach something about the landscape. Do not trespass or create a safety problem just to get a radio test.

  • Public parks and overlooks.
  • Library or community center parking lots.
  • Hilltops and ridgelines where public access is allowed.
  • Downtown streets and dense neighborhoods.
  • Road corridors and river corridors.
  • Workplaces or campuses where you have permission to test.
  • Places where communication would be useful during an outage or event.

Testing from ordinary places matters. A network is only useful if people understand how it behaves where they actually live, work, travel, and gather.

Terrain and Placement

Radio signals are often better with height and clearer paths. A small change in placement can make a large difference.

  • A node near a window may work better than one deep inside a building.
  • A hilltop may hear more than a valley.
  • A mag-mount antenna on a vehicle roof may beat a handheld inside the car.
  • Trees, wet leaves, brick, metal, and concrete can reduce signal quality.
  • Outdoor repeaters need careful thought about weather, power, and mounting.

When a test works, try to write down why it may have worked. When a test fails, write down what may have blocked it.

Privacy and Safety

Coverage reports are meant to help the community, not expose people’s private lives. Keep reports useful without oversharing.

  • Do not post someone else’s private address.
  • Do not publish exact home locations unless the owner clearly wants that shared.
  • Use general areas when possible.
  • Do not trespass for a test.
  • Do not block roads, drive distracted, or create a public safety issue.
  • Follow the rules for whatever radio service you are using.

A good community map does not need to expose every person or every exact location. It needs enough information to understand coverage.

Sharing Results

Share coverage reports in the community Discord or by email. Over time, reports can be turned into better public notes, maps, guides, and planning for future nodes or repeaters.

Good First Coverage Goals

  • Test from one public place near you.
  • Write down what device and antenna you used.
  • Note whether you heard any known nodes, repeaters, or stations.
  • Compare two nearby locations, such as a low spot and a higher spot.
  • Share one short report with the community.
  • Repeat the same test later after settings, antennas, or node locations change.

Small repeatable tests are better than vague claims. The goal is to build a local record that becomes more useful over time.
