Share what helps explain the problem

Good troubleshooting information usually includes the device, firmware, antenna, settings, general location, and what happened. It rarely requires an exact private address, account details, or a complete unedited screenshot.

Do not post passwords, private keys, access tokens, broker credentials, private messages, or exact private locations without permission.

Before posting, ask whether each visible detail helps another person understand the equipment or result. Crop or remove anything that does not.

Useful information to include

Enough technical context makes it easier for other people to help without requiring unnecessary personal information.

Device
The radio model, firmware version, companion application, and any relevant hardware revision.
Radio setup
Antenna type, connector or adapter, power source, and whether the device was indoors, outdoors, in a vehicle, or mounted higher up.
Settings
The selected region, preset, frequency, role, channel, or other setting directly related to the question.
General area
A town, neighborhood, public landmark, road corridor, hill, or broad testing area is usually enough.
What happened
Describe what you expected, what actually happened, and whether the device heard any contacts, adverts, messages, or packets.
What you tried
Mention restarts, firmware changes, alternate antennas, different locations, or settings you already checked.
Change one thing at a time while troubleshooting. A clear sequence of tests is usually more useful than a long list of settings changed at once.

Check screenshots and photos

Images often include more information than the person posting them notices at first.

Screenshots

  • Crop the image to the part that matters.
  • Remove passwords, keys, tokens, and broker credentials.
  • Check private messages and contact names.
  • Check coordinates and saved locations.
  • Look for account names, email addresses, and profile details.
  • Check browser tabs, bookmarks, notifications, and filenames.
  • Inspect QR codes before posting them.

Photographs

  • Check addresses, house numbers, and street signs.
  • Check license plates, badges, labels, and paperwork.
  • Look at screens, windows, mirrors, and other reflections.
  • Consider whether the surroundings reveal a private node location.
  • Make sure visible people are comfortable being posted.
  • Use a closer equipment photo when the wider setting is unnecessary.

Files can contain hidden details

A photo, exported configuration, log, or diagnostic file may contain more information than appears on the screen.

Photo metadata
Some image files may include capture time, device information, or GPS coordinates. Remove unnecessary metadata before publicly posting photos taken at a private location.
Configuration exports
Review exported settings for channel keys, Wi-Fi credentials, broker details, device secrets, and administrative information.
Logs
Logs may contain node identifiers, usernames, filesystem paths, IP addresses, hostnames, coordinates, or message contents.
QR codes
A QR code may contain a channel configuration, key, invitation, Wi-Fi password, or another value that is not obvious from the image alone.

When possible, copy only the relevant lines into a message rather than uploading an entire configuration or log file.

Choose the right amount of location detail

The appropriate level of detail depends on the location and why it is being discussed.

General area A town or broad area such as “Southwick,” “near downtown Springfield,” or “the Monson area” is usually enough for initial troubleshooting.
Public testing location A park, trail, public event, road overlook, or other clearly public location can often be identified directly.
Private installation Avoid posting an address, exact coordinates, roof access information, or detailed directions unless the location is intentionally public and the property owner has agreed.
Third-party location Do not publish someone else’s home, workplace, node location, or travel pattern merely because it appeared in an application or packet.
A precise location may be appropriate for a public event or an intentionally documented public node. It is not automatically appropriate for every fixed device visible on the mesh.

Node names and identifiers

A useful node name helps people understand what they are seeing without unnecessarily identifying a person or private address.

  • Use a short, recognizable name.
  • A town, broad area, hill, park, or public landmark may be useful.
  • Add a role such as repeater, room, observer, or tracker when it helps.
  • Avoid addresses, apartment numbers, phone numbers, or full personal names.
  • Do not assume another operator wants their node name connected publicly to their identity.

Node identifiers, public keys, hashes, and packet details may be technically visible over the radio. That does not necessarily mean they need to be collected into a public profile of the person operating the device.

Public channels should be treated as public

Messages sent through public MeshCore channels or the default public Meshtastic channel may be received, repeated, logged, or viewed outside the immediate area.

Shared-key channels limit who can read the message contents, but anyone with the configuration can participate. They should not be treated as a safe place for passwords, credentials, highly sensitive personal information, or anything that would cause serious harm if exposed.

Keep routine tests short and use the locally recommended test channel where one is available. Avoid repeatedly filling a broad public channel with test messages.

Maps and observed activity

The Hampden County Mesh live map and activity analyzer display MeshCore activity heard through participating observer systems.

A visible node or packet does not mean Hampden County Mesh owns the device, knows who operates it, or can confirm that another radio can reach it directly.

Publicly visible map data also does not make unrelated private information fair game. Avoid combining a marker with names, addresses, photographs, property records, or other information to identify a private operator.

Choose the right place to share it

Discord Device help, setup questions, local observations, community discussion, and ordinary troubleshooting. GitHub Website corrections, broken links, documentation changes, and public repository issues. Email Outreach, events, private questions, property discussions, or information that should not begin in a public channel.

Report security issues privately

Do not open a public Discord or GitHub discussion containing exposed credentials, private keys, access tokens, administrative access details, or instructions that could help someone disrupt a system.

Preserve enough information to explain the issue, but redact credentials and unrelated personal data before sending it.

Security contact

Send reports involving HCM systems, credentials, or infrastructure access directly to the project.

security@hampdencountymesh.org

One last check

  1. Does the post include enough information to understand the question?
  2. Does it expose a credential, key, private message, or exact private location?
  3. Does the image show unrelated people, accounts, notifications, or surroundings?
  4. Could a general area describe the test just as well as precise coordinates?
  5. Does the file contain metadata or hidden configuration details?
  6. Are you sharing someone else’s information without their permission?

When a detail does not help explain the equipment, test, or problem, leave it out.
