Radio can be confusing because different services have different rules, equipment, ranges, habits, and communities. This page gives a simple overview so beginners can understand the difference between mesh devices, handheld radios, scanners, and licensed radio services.

This page is not legal advice and does not replace FCC rules, manufacturer documentation, or current official guidance. Rules can change, and members are responsible for using the correct equipment and following the rules for the service they use.

For detailed rules, use official FCC and eCFR sources linked near the bottom of this page.

QUICK COMPARISON

MeshCore

A LoRa-based mesh messaging system used by several nearby mesh communities. It is the main mesh focus of this site.

Useful for local off-grid messaging, repeaters, observers, and learning how small mesh devices behave around local terrain.

Meshtastic

A LoRa-based mesh messaging project with broad hardware and app support. It is separate from MeshCore but useful for many of the same local learning goals.

Useful for portable nodes, field testing, mapping experiments, and comparing mesh behavior.

FRS

Family Radio Service. Common license-by-rule handheld radios often used for short-range local voice communication.

Useful for simple local voice use when compliant FRS radios are used.

GMRS

General Mobile Radio Service. A personal radio service that requires an FCC license and is commonly used with handhelds, mobiles, and repeaters.

Useful for local and family voice communication, especially where repeaters or better antennas are available.

MURS

Multi-Use Radio Service. A license-by-rule VHF radio service used for short-distance voice and data applications with compliant radios.

Useful for simple local communication where MURS fits the need.

CB

Citizens Band radio. A long-running personal radio service often associated with mobile, highway, hobby, and local voice use.

Useful for learning basic radio behavior on lower frequencies and for occasional local or mobile monitoring.

Amateur radio

A licensed radio service for technical learning, experimentation, public service, repeaters, digital modes, and radio communication.

Useful for deeper radio learning, repeater use, antennas, emergency communications training, and technical experimentation.

Scanners

Receive-only radios used to listen to public, amateur, aviation, railroad, weather, business, or other radio traffic where lawful.

Useful for learning local radio activity without transmitting.

VOICE RADIO BASICS

Voice radio services are useful for local communication, but they are not interchangeable. A radio that can physically tune a frequency is not automatically legal or appropriate to transmit there.

Before transmitting, make sure the radio service, radio equipment, frequency, antenna, power level, and license status match the rules for that service.

Listening is different from transmitting, but local, state, and federal rules can still matter depending on the service, context, and how information is used.

FRS

Good for simple short-range handheld voice communication with compliant FRS radios.

It is usually the easiest place for beginners to understand basic push-to-talk radio use.

GMRS

Useful for handhelds, mobile radios, and repeaters when used under the correct license and rules.

GMRS can be a practical local voice option for families, groups, vehicles, events, and community coordination.

MURS

A VHF personal radio service that can be useful for local voice communication with compliant radios.

It may behave differently from FRS or GMRS because of frequency, antennas, terrain, and nearby noise.

CB

A familiar radio service often used from vehicles or base stations.

CB can behave very differently from small UHF handhelds and is a useful way to learn how frequency and antenna size affect radio use.

Amateur radio

Amateur radio requires passing a license exam before transmitting, but it opens the door to repeaters, digital modes, antennas, public service, and much deeper radio experimentation.

It is often the best path for people who want to understand radio beyond simple consumer use.

Scanners and receive-only listening

Scanners are useful for learning what radio activity exists nearby without transmitting.

They can help beginners understand repeaters, dispatch channels, aviation, railroads, weather radio, and other local radio activity.

LOCAL RADIO LEARNING
Local terrain, tower sites, hills, valleys, buildings, and tree cover all affect radio behavior.

Radio is local.

Hampden County has hills, valleys, river corridors, dense city blocks, wooded areas, industrial buildings, highways, farms, and high spots like Mount Tom. All of that changes how radios behave.

A handheld radio, LoRa node, mobile antenna, scanner, or repeater may work very differently depending on height, placement, terrain, foliage, building materials, and nearby interference.

The best way to learn is to try real places, compare notes, and keep public notes general enough to protect privacy.

RESPONSIBLE USE

Know before transmitting

Make sure you know which radio service you are using before you transmit. Different services have different rules, equipment requirements, and license expectations.

Use the right equipment

Radios should be appropriate for the service being used. A radio being technically capable of transmitting somewhere does not mean it is allowed there.

Do not interfere

Do not jam, interfere, impersonate, transmit on emergency or public safety systems, or use radio systems in a way that creates a safety problem.

Protect privacy

Do not publish private addresses, private node locations, private coordinates, credentials, private messages, or sensitive screenshots without permission.
